Oil and Gas Deaths: Part 4

Limitations keep Colorado’s oil commission from having greater role in safety

State agency is about protecting environment and general public, not industry workers

Published September 29, 2016

By Monte Whaley and John Ingold

Colorado oil and gas regulators receive dozens of reports about workplace accidents every year. But the regulators, lacking legislative authority, don’t punish companies with repeated worker safety problems or share the reports with federal safety officials. And the majority of deaths in the industry go undocumented by the regulators, The Denver Post has found.

That gap is just one example of a missed opportunity by state leaders to better understand occupational safety on oil and gas sites and to protect workers.

In Colorado, there is no commission devoted solely to worker safety, no agency that warns lawmakers about trends in worker deaths and no researchers who cull data on all workplace injuries.

The state, for instance, is one of only nine not participating in a federal survey that tracks when workers are hurt but not killed. The federal government provides a grant to pay for the state’s collection of data on worker deaths. But the Colorado legislature must pay half the cost of participating in the nationwide Survey of Occupational Injuries and Illnesses.

And that hasn’t happened.

“It’s cost-prohibitive for Colorado to participate in that process,” said Michael Van Dyke, a Colorado Health Department official who oversees the state’s collection of reports on workplace deaths.

Other states have been more proactive when it comes to monitoring worker safety, especially on oil and gas sites.

Wyoming has a researcher who does nothing but track workplace deaths and who produces an annual report identifying trends and recommending changes.

West Virginia formed a commission to study worker safety in the oil and gas industry. The commission’s work led the state legislature to pass a bill this year that will create a database of accidents at oil and gas worksites.

In Colorado, there already is a requirement that oil and gas companies report certain accidents to the Colorado Oil and Gas Conservation Commission, the state agency that regulates the industry. But state law says the COGCC should administer rules only to protect the environment and the general public. The statute specifically forbids the COGCC from imposing worker safety rules on companies already regulated by the Occupational Health and Safety Administration.

“I think we view our authority in this realm pretty narrowly,” said Matt Lepore, the commission’s director.

Between 2000 and 2015, companies reported 485 accidents to the COGCC, using a document that the commission calls a Form 22. In 348 of those accidents, a worker was injured or killed, a Post review of the reports found.

But the complicated wording of the rule that requires the reports gives companies wide discretion about when — and when not — to tell state regulators about an accident.

COGCC Rule 602(b) says companies must report accidents that result in “injury to a member of the general public which requires Medical Treatment [sic]” or that cause “significant damage” to equipment. Additional wording in the rule narrows the requirement even further. The only accidents that need to be reported are those “involving a fire, explosion, detonation, or release of pressure.”

Lepore said most companies err on the side of overreporting, something the COGCC encourages.

The Post, in reviewing the reports, found instances when companies told the COGCC about workers who suffered bee stings, split lips, twisted ankles or pinched fingers that needed stitches — all things they were not technically obligated to report.

But the limited nature of the rule also gives companies latitude not to report serious accidents or deaths.

Long day on rig has chilling consequences

Jeremy Barnhart worked through pain but became victim of frostbite

By Monte Whaley, The Denver Post

Getting up each day and working hard seems etched in Jeremy Barnhart’s genetic code, and it led him to a life working in the oil patch.

“I just like to work. My dad worked hard, and he always said, ‘Get to work and get the job done,’ ” said Barnhart, 44. “It just comes naturally. And I always had real physical jobs.”

But on a frigid day last December, Barnhart’s life collided head-on with the realities of laboring in oil and gas extraction.

Barnhart was working a 12-hour shift at a remote site in northwestern Wyoming when he felt horrific pain in his feet. Then it all seemed to go away.

The weather that day was bitter: The temperature never got above 25 degrees and bottomed out just above zero, and the Wyoming wind added to the misery, blowing 20 to 30 mph out of the north.

Later when he tried to pull off his left boot, Barnhart knew something was wrong.

“It looked sloped, kind of like it had been inside a wooden shoe,” said Barnhart. “I didn’t know what to think. I had never heard of anything like this ever happening and surely not to me.”

Barnhart was initially seen at a hospital in Wyoming and then flown to the University of Colorado Hospital Burn Center in Aurora to be treated for severe frostbite, arriving 12 hours after he noticed his feet were turning purple.

Barnhart was admitted Dec. 16, and three weeks later, his left foot and leg were amputated just below the knee. Also, all the toes on his right foot were removed.

He also began a demanding schedule of rehabilitation, made a little easier by his upbeat attitude and the care from the burn center’s doctors and nurses.

“His outlook has been wonderful,” said Dr. Anne Wagner, interim director of the burn center. “He has taken everything in stride and is looking to his future.”

Barnhart said he won’t let this get him down, and he is confident he can adjust to a different lifestyle.

“There is still a lot of life to be lived,” he said during his rehab. “I just have to readjust to working someplace else, maybe in an office.”

Barnhart’s experience with frostbite is fairly common. It happens when a person’s skin and deeper tissues freeze because of prolonged exposure to cold temperatures and high winds.

To fight the cold, the body will reduce blood flow to a person’s extremities to try to maintain a core body temperature.

“Usually, frostbite victims feel some severe pain at first and then the feet feel fine,” Wagner said.

By the time he arrived at the CU hospital, his feet had been “rewarmed.” All patients who suffer severe frostbite are rewarmed quickly, usually within 30-40 minutes, at 40 degrees Celsius (104 Fahrenheit).

“The sooner we can get to the patient from the time of the rewarming to the start of the (drug treatments), … the better the outcome,” Wagner said.

In Barnhart’s case, after rewarming, “we knew there was likely to be an amputation,” Wagner said.

After the Jan. 8 surgery, Barnhart underwent months of physical therapy to prepare for life away from the oil fields.

“It’s funny. Just about all my life I only cared about the price of oil — ‘Is it up? Is it down?’ ” said Barnhart. “But now, I’ve got to think about doing something else.”

Searching through government documents and news reports, The Post found records on 44 workplace deaths connected to the oil and gas industry in Colorado since 2000. The federal Bureau of Labor Statistics says there have been even more — reporting 51 deaths in Colorado’s oil and gas industry between 2003 and 2014.

The COGCC, though, has received Form 22s for only 18 deaths since 2000.

The large majority of unreported deaths — as well as a number of the reported deaths — were the result of accidents that companies were not officially required to tell the COGCC about. But there were also two deaths involving releases of pressure, one each in 2000 and 2014, for which no Form 22 could be found.

Lepore said he was aware of the 2014 death, which involved a Halliburton employee named Matt Smith who was working on a site owned by Anadarko Petroleum. When told no report about Smith’s death could be found, Lepore expressed confusion.

“I know about that incident, and, if there’s not a Form 22, I am a little bit surprised,” he said.

A subsequent check by COGCC staff failed to turn up the report. An Anadarko representative said the report was Halliburton’s responsibility, and a Halliburton spokeswoman said it was Anadarko’s.

A spokesman for the commission later wrote in an e-mail that companies sometimes notify the COGCC of deaths through phone calls but don’t file a Form 22. He said the commission’s staff “cannot readily recall a case involving a fatality where COGCC was not notified in some way.”

“From our experience, it’s true, at times, that filing a form with an agency that doesn’t regulate workplace safety has been lost in the trauma of the event,” spokesman Todd Hartman wrote in the e-mail.

COGCC rules allow the commission to punish an oil and gas company with a fine of up to $10,000 a day for not filing an accident report, but Lepore said the commission would typically just ask the company to correct the lapse by filing a new report. Hartman said the commission’s staff is currently developing clearer guidance for when a Form 22 is required.

Even when regulators receive a Form 22, their focus isn’t on worker safety.

In 2012, for instance, an explosion at a well in Weld County killed Brian Wallace, a subcontractor at the well site. When the site’s operator, Encana, reported the explosion — and the subsequent release of natural gas — to the COGCC, it referenced Wallace’s death tersely.

“The incident resulted in one contractor fatality, and three other injuries,” the company wrote.

A COGCC manager didn’t ask for more details.

“No further action is required for reporting on the accident portion,” the manager wrote to Encana in an e-mail. “The COGCC environmental group will be in contact if additional information is needed on the release.”

Lepore said the COGCC collects Form 22s as a way to monitor emerging environmental safety hazards at oil and gas sites. For instance, he mentioned a rash of fires at oil-collection tanks in 2014 that COGCC staffers first noticed in Form 22s. The COGCC asked one company to do a deeper search for the cause.

The answer, though, may have provided a clue to a problem federal occupational safety researchers, too, were wrestling with.

In 2014, three workers died of heart failure in Colorado after breathing in toxic fumes while taking samples from the same type of collection tanks, a process known as “tank gauging.” The COGCC, in fact, had received a Form 22 for one of those deaths, as well as another Form 22 for an instance when a worker “lost and regained consciousness” while tank gauging.

Lepore said one possible explanation for the fires was the linking up of multiple collection tanks, something that has become more common as companies use new technology to drill several wells from the same pad. The linking allows large concentrations of fumes from the whole series of tanks to escape from a single leakage point — or a single sampling hatch.

COGCC staffers have considered proposing a rule requiring there to be valves between the tanks to prevent the buildup of fumes, Lepore said. But, because of the COGCC’s limitations when it comes to worker safety, the change would be intended to address only the fires, not the deaths.

“That’s not our role,” he said.

Instead, Lepore said, it’s OSHA’s.

But, as with all industries, oil and gas companies are required to call OSHA after a small percentage of only the most serious accidents that occur on worksites. A new rule will require companies with more than 250 employees to submit data on all accidents to OSHA four times a year. Smaller companies in some industries — but not the oil and gas industry — will also have to submit the data.

That makes the COGCC’s Form 22s a potentially valuable source of information on accidents at oil and gas sites. But Lepore said he knows of no instance when the COGCC shared information or trends from the accident reports with OSHA. Herb Gibson, OSHA’s area director in Denver, said he can’t recall any time when the COGCC notified his agency of safety problems.

Other states, when faced with deaths in the oil and gas industry, have decided it’s not enough to leave worker safety solely up to OSHA.

In Wyoming, where the Bureau of Labor Statistics says 69 oil and gas workers died between 2003 and 2014, officials in 2011 created a position for a state occupational epidemiologist — a researcher who would examine trends in oil and gas deaths and recommend changes that could prevent them. The epidemiologist now produces a report every year that goes to lawmakers and state officials.

Meredith Towle, the current epidemiologist, said she has changed the way some deaths are categorized — for example, putting the deaths of workers driving between wells into the oil and gas classification instead of the transportation classification — to get employers to pay more attention to the industry’s hazards.

“We just wanted the oil and gas industry to get a better picture of what is causing fatalities among the workers supporting the industry,” she said.

In West Virginia, where 26 oil and gas workers died between 2003 and 2014, Gov. Earl Ray Tomblin was so alarmed at the accidents — and the fact that he learned about many of them only through the media — that he has created a special commission on oil and gas safety. The commission included state and local officials and representatives of the industry, held six meetings across the state in 2015 and, by the end, presented 18 recommendations on worker safety.

Among the proposals: a comprehensive database of fatal and serious-injury accidents at oil and gas sites, a centralized list of classes on occupational safety, a set of best practices for monitoring toxic vapors at worksites, and a legal change that might encourage companies to investigate more deeply after an accident.

The West Virginia legislature passed bills this year that adopted several of the recommendations, including a reporting requirement that will lead to the database’s creation.

“These additional enhancements will make the operation of this entire industry a safer one for the workers,” said Lawrence Malone, the chairman of the commission and Tomblin’s policy director.

That, said Peter Dooley, a safety advocate with the National Council for Occupational Safety and Health, is really the goal: incremental improvement. And, with the oil and gas industry, that improvement often comes first through just learning about every accident and injury. When worker safety lives in the shadows, he said, dangers thrive.

“Ultimately,” Dooley said, “oil and gas is a very, very, very hazardous industry.”
